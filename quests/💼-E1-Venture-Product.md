## 📖 Theory Bite

| 🧩 Concept | 💡 What it means |
|---------|---------------|
| 🎯 Problem-first thinking | A good problem > a good idea — problems persist, ideas fade |
| 🗺️ Opportunity framing | Who has the problem? How painful? How many? How much would they pay? |
| ⏰ Market timing | Being early = being wrong. Being late = being irrelevant. The window matters. |
| 🎨 Positioning | How you describe your product determines who believes it's for them |

> 💼 **Daily reality:** Talking to users, writing problem statements, mapping markets, and killing your own bad ideas before someone else does.

## ⚔️ Quests

### 🗝️ Quest E1.1 — Opportunity Map
**⏱ Time:** ~45 min | **Difficulty:** ⭐ Beginner

**Quest:** Turn a vague idea into a structured opportunity.

```
OPPORTUNITY MAP
├── 💡 The vague idea: __________________
├── 🎯 Problem: What's broken?
├── 👥 Affected users: Who suffers?
├── 🔄 Current alternatives: What do they use now?
├── ❌ Why alternatives fail: Where's the gap?
├── 🔑 Your wedge: Why you?
└── 📏 One success metric
```

> 💭 **Reflection:** Was it painful to kill parts of your idea? That's the point.

---

### 🗝️ Quest E1.2 — Problem Statement
**⏱ Time:** ~30 min | **Difficulty:** ⭐ Beginner

**Quest:** Write a crisp problem statement using a real domain.

**Format:**
> [Who] struggles with [what] because [why]. Current solutions fail because [gap]. If solved, [impact].

**Example:**
> Remote teams of 5-20 people struggle with silent conflict because there's no structured way to surface tensions. Current tools (Slack polls, 1-on-1s) fail because they rely on people self-reporting. If solved, team productivity could increase 20-40%.

> 💭 **Reflection:** Did you want to jump to the solution, or did the problem framing feel valuable?

---

### 🗝️ Quest E1.3 — Idea vs. Problem vs. Timing
**⏱ Time:** ~30 min | **Difficulty:** ⭐ Beginner

**Quest:** Understand which factor matters most.

**Steps:**
1. Find one real example for each:

| Factor | Example | Outcome |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Good idea, bad problem | | |
| Good problem, bad timing | | |
| Good timing, wrong idea | | |

2. Rank them by importance: ___ > ___ > ___
3. Explain your ranking in 3 sentences

> 💭 **Reflection:** Do you naturally think about timing and market conditions, or just the product?

---

### 🗝️ Quest E1.4 — Idea Graveyard
**⏱ Time:** ~20 min | **Difficulty:** ⭐ Beginner

**Quest:** Practice killing your own ideas. The best founders do this fast.

**Steps:**
1. List 3 startup ideas you've had (or make up 3)
2. For each, write one sentence on why it might fail
3. Rate each idea's survivability:

| Idea | Why it might fail | Survivability (1-5) |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| | | |
| | | |
| | | |

**Rule:** Be honest. A killed idea frees energy for a better one.

### 🗝️ Quest E1.5 — Market Sizing
**⏱ Time:** ~60 min | **Difficulty:** ⭐⭐⭐ Advanced

**Quest:** Estimate the market size for one product idea.

**Steps:**
1. Pick one product idea
2. Calculate:

| Metric | Value | How you estimated |
|--------|-------|-------------------|
| TAM (Total Addressable Market) | | Everyone who could use this |
| SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) | | The subset you can reach |
| SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) | | What you can realistically capture in 2 years |
| Price per customer | | |
| **SOM revenue** | **$___** | SOM × price |

3. Is this market big enough to sustain a venture? What's the minimum viable market?

<details>
<summary>� Quick method: Top-down vs. Bottom-up</summary>

**Top-down:** "The X market is $10B. If we capture 1%, that's $100M."
→ Fast but often delusional.

**Bottom-up:** "There are 50,000 potential customers. At $200/year, that's $10M. If we convert 5%, that's $500K."
→ Slower but more honest.

Always do bottom-up. Use top-down only as a sanity check.

</details>
