## How These Tasks Work

Each section has three layers:

1. **Theory bite** — just enough context to understand what you're doing
2. **Practice tasks** — hands-on micro-projects (not quizzes)
3. **Test-drive question** — would you do this daily?

## `E1` · Venture / Product / Opportunity

### Theory

Venture and product work is about **finding problems worth solving and framing them as opportunities**. Key concepts:

- **Problem-first thinking**: a good problem is more valuable than a good idea
- **Opportunity framing**: who has the problem? how painful is it? how many people have it? how much would they pay?
- **Market timing**: being early = being wrong. Being late = being irrelevant. The window matters.
- **Positioning**: how you describe your product determines who believes it's for them

Daily reality: talking to users, writing problem statements, mapping markets, and killing your own bad ideas before someone else does.

### Practice Tasks

1. **Opportunity map** — Turn one vague startup idea into a structured opportunity map: problem, affected users, current alternatives, why they fail, your wedge, and one metric for success.

2. **Problem statement** — Write a one-page problem statement using a real domain example. Format: "[Who] struggles with [what] because [why]. Current solutions fail because [gap]."

3. **Idea vs. problem vs. timing** — Compare "good idea" vs "good problem" vs "good market timing" with one real example for each. Which matters most? Why?

4. **Idea graveyard** — List 3 startup ideas you've had. For each, write one sentence on why it might fail. Be honest. The best founders kill their own ideas fast.

5. **Market sizing** — Pick one product idea and estimate the market size: TAM (total addressable), SAM (serviceable), SOM (obtainable). Show your math and assumptions.

### Test-Drive Question

Can you spend your day talking to people about their problems, killing ideas that don't hold up, and staying motivated even when the data says "not yet"?

## `E2` · Validation / GTM / Market

### Theory

Validation and go-to-market are about **testing whether people actually want what you'd build**. Key concepts:

- **Customer discovery**: before building, talk to 20 potential users. Not friends.
- **Validation loop**: hypothesis → test → learn → pivot or persevere
- **GTM channels**: how do users find you? (search, social, sales, partnerships, communities)
- **Messaging hierarchy**: who it's for → what it does → why it's different → proof

Daily reality: writing interview scripts, running experiments, reading analytics, and accepting that most of your assumptions are wrong.

### Practice Tasks

1. **Validation checklist** — Build a first-pass validation checklist for a chosen idea: 10 questions you need answered before building. Rank them by "how easy is this to test?"

2. **Interview script** — Draft 5 customer interview questions that are actually useful. Rule: no leading questions, no "would you use this?" — only "tell me about the last time you..."

3. **GTM map** — Map one startup idea into `audience → need → message → first channel`. Be specific: name the channel, write the message, define the audience in one sentence.

4. **Landing page test** — Write copy for a fake landing page (no product yet). The goal: would someone give you their email? Include: headline, 3 benefits, one proof point, CTA.

5. **Competitive audit** — Find 3 existing products in your idea's space. For each: what they do well, what they miss, and one thing you'd do differently.

### Test-Drive Question

Can you handle the emotional cycle of: excitement → testing → "we were wrong" → new hypothesis → repeat?

## `E3` · Ecosystem / Experts / Partners

### Theory

Ecosystem work is about **building the network that makes your venture possible**. Key concepts:

- **Stakeholder mapping**: who matters for your success? (investors, advisors, partners, regulators, community)
- **Social capital**: relationships are assets — invest before you need them
- **Strategic partnerships**: who already has the users/data/distribution you need?
- **Expert leverage**: you don't need to know everything — you need to know who knows

Daily reality: networking (yes, really), writing outreach messages, attending events, and maintaining relationships with no immediate payoff.

### Practice Tasks

1. **Ecosystem map** — Build a mini ecosystem map around one direction. Include: 5 key players, their roles, and how they connect to each other.

2. **Support registry entry** — Add one useful startup, expert, or ally to a shared registry. Include: name, what they do, why they're relevant, and how to reach them.

3. **"Who should I talk to first?" note** — Draft a guide for a founder entering your domain: 3 people to talk to, 3 communities to join, 3 events to attend.

4. **Outreach draft** — Write a cold outreach message to one potential advisor or partner. Under 100 words. Make it about them, not you.

5. **Partnership proposal** — Draft a one-page partnership proposal between two companies. What does each give? What does each get? Why now?

### Test-Drive Question

Can you invest in relationships that may not pay off for months — and enjoy the process of connecting people, not just using them?

## `E4` · Public Cases / Founder Ops

### Theory

Founder operations is about **running the venture like a system, not a hustle**. Key concepts:

- **Decision frameworks**: build/wait/kill — explicit criteria prevent emotional decisions
- **Communication rhythm**: weekly updates keep investors, team, and advisors aligned
- **Case documentation**: your story is an asset — public cases attract talent, users, and capital
- **Operational discipline**: what gets tracked gets managed

Daily reality: writing updates, tracking metrics, making hard decisions with incomplete data, and documenting everything so others can learn from your journey.

### Practice Tasks

1. **Weekly update template** — Write a founder update template with sections for: wins, blockers, key metric, next week's focus, and one ask.

2. **Public case structure** — Convert one founder story (real or hypothetical) into a structured case: background, decision, outcome, lesson. Keep it under 500 words.

3. **Build/wait/kill board** — Create a lean decision board for an MVP idea. For each option: criteria, evidence needed, decision deadline, and who decides.

4. **Metric dashboard** — Design a one-page dashboard for a startup. Include: 5 key metrics, their targets, current values, and trend arrows. No vanity metrics allowed.

5. **Post-mortem** — Write a post-mortem for a failed feature or product (real or imagined). Format: what we expected, what happened, why, what we'd do differently.

### Test-Drive Question

Can you maintain operational discipline — tracking, updating, documenting — even when it feels like nobody is reading?
